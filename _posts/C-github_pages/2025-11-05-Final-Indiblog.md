# Reflecting on My Computer Science Journey

## Growth from the Beginning of the Year

Looking back at where I started this year, I've grown significantly in how I approach challenges. I've learned to **fail fast** - instead of spending endless hours trying to make something perfect, I now experiment quickly, learn from what doesn't work, and move on. I've become more **patient** with the learning process, understanding that mastery takes time. Most importantly, I've shifted my mindset to actively **look for solutions** rather than dwelling on problems. When something breaks or doesn't work, I now ask "how can I fix this?" instead of getting stuck on "why isn't this working?"

## Key Takeaways from the Past 3 Sprints

### Tools: Setting Up for Success

This trimester, I got a new computer and had to set everything up from scratch. Instead of seeing it as a setback, I turned it into an opportunity to **gamify my tool setup**. I challenged myself to see how quickly I could get my development environment running, and I took part in some incredible hacks along the way. Having the right tools configured efficiently has made such a difference in my productivity - what used to take me 20 minutes now takes 10.

### Lessons: The Art of Teaching

When it came time to give lessons to the class, I learned that **the hook is everything**. You have to grab people's attention in the first 30 seconds or you'll lose them. I focused on making lessons quick and engaging, spending less time on lengthy explanations and more time on **hands-on classwork**. People learn by doing, not by watching someone talk for 30 minutes. The feedback I got showed that interactive lessons stick way better than passive ones.

### Digital Famine & N@tM: Finding Inspiration

The Digital Famine experience and Night at the Museum were eye-opening. Seeing what other people created was **genuinely inspiring** - the creativity and technical skill on display pushed me to think bigger about my own projects. I also learned the value of **giving constructive feedback**. When you're thoughtful about what you share with others about their work, it helps both them and you think more critically about design and implementation choices.

## What I Want to Learn Next in CompSci

Moving forward, I have some clear goals for how I want to improve my collaboration skills:

- **Stop latching onto each other** - I've noticed our group tends to work too closely together sometimes, which means we're not covering as much ground as we could
- **Talk across the table** - Communication shouldn't just be with the person next to you. We need to get better at sharing ideas with the whole team
- **Don't let people take over** - I want to be more conscious about making sure everyone's voice is heard and no single person dominates the direction of a project
